The Athletic Hip and Groin
Without sufficient strength, mobility and neuromuscular control, the hip and groin are vulnerable 
to injury. Injuries in these regions range from acute strains to chronic, overuse-related pathologies. 
For athletes, developing and maintaining robust hip function is not only essential for enhancing 
their performance – it can also be a critical component in their transition from injury back to full 
participation, from “patient” to “performer.”
…maintaining robust hip function is…a critical component in  
transitioning from injury back to full participation…
The ability of clinicians to monitor strength, mobility and neuromuscular control of the athletic hip  
and groin is critical. Whether evaluating the force production capacity of a muscle group,  
closed-environment biomechanics or sport-specific jump performance, the regular, reliable and objective 
monitoring of these tasks is crucial to keeping the athletic hip and groin injury-free and ready to perform. 
Pain and dysfunction in the hip and groin regions can have a profound impact on an athlete’s 
performance, longevity and quality of life. A comprehensive understanding of hip and groin 
biomechanics, common injury presentations and evidence-based assessment strategies is essential 
for effective injury evaluation, diagnosis and rehabilitation in athletic populations.
The Aging Hip
Pain is not only a limiting factor for elite athletes. Chronic degenerative changes and injury can 
leave lasting impacts on anyone, with nearly 30% of adults over 55 suffering from symptomatic hip 
osteoarthritis (OA) (Fu et al., 2022). 
While the forces experienced at the hip joint during daily activities are significantly lower than those  
experienced during high-performance sport-specific tasks, even subtle changes in balance or functional 
capacity – often undetectable without objective tools – can signal the early stages of dysfunction.  
High-fidelity technologies like force plates can help uncover these changes, providing valuable  
insight into functional decline and enabling earlier, more targeted interventions, regardless of age  
or activity level.

demarcated by three anatomical landmarks: the anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS), the pubic tubercle 
and the 3G point (midway between the ASIS and the top of the patella).

Articular Structures
The primary joint in the hip complex is the femoroacetabular joint (FAJ), a large ball-and-socket joint 
essential for lower-body mobility and stability. While other joints, such as the sacroiliac, contribute to 
pelvic function, they are difficult to assess objectively and are excluded from this guide. 
The FAJ is built to withstand high compressive forces. Without proper conditioning, these forces –
combined with factors like genetics, metabolic syndrome and injury history – can lead to cartilage 
damage and OA. 
The joint is stabilized passively by a strong fibrous capsule reinforced by ligaments and dynamically 
stabilized by muscles such as the iliacus and psoas major. The labrum, a ring of fibrocartilage, 
deepens the socket, improves load distribution, maintains joint congruency and supports cartilage 
health. However, it is prone to tears and impingement, which can disrupt hip function.
The pubic symphysis, a fibrocartilaginous joint, also contributes to hip stability by transferring force 
between the legs.

Applications of Consensus Statements
The complex anatomy of the hip and groin regions presents a difficult clinical challenge for 
practitioners searching for a single structural cause of pain and dysfunction. 
To address this problem, consensus meetings such as the “Doha Agreement meeting on terminology  
and definitions in groin pain in athletes” and the “Warwick Agreement on FAI syndrome” have 
established clinical classification systems based on patient-reported injury history and clinical 
examination findings. 
These classification systems help to ensure consistent treatment strategies are followed to optimize 
hip and groin rehabilitation. 
Repetitive impingement of the femoral neck against the acetabular rim is the leading hypothesis for 
chondrolabral damage, predisposing individuals to the premature development of OA.
Management strategies vary from conservative to operative, depending on clinical severity. 
Long-term monitoring and movement assessments are essential to track functional progress and 
prevent recurrent symptoms.

Hip and Groin Assessment
Assessing hip function is essential for optimizing movement efficiency, reducing injury risk and guiding 
rehabilitation and training programs. The hip joint plays a central role in load distribution, force genera-
tion and mobility, particularly in athletes and active individuals.  
Identifying strength impairments, asymmetries and mobility limitations allows for the implementation of 
targeted impairment-based interventions that can enhance performance and mitigate the risk of injuries.

devices that measure internal pressure and are 
often used as low-tech biofeedback tools.

Isokinetic Dynamometers (IKDs)
IKDs have long been used to measure hip torque and muscle strength across different contraction types  
(isometric, concentric, eccentric). 
These devices provide precise kinetic data but are often impractical for day-to-day clinical use due to their 
expense, size and complexity of test set-ups, especially when assessing the force production capacities 
of the hip musculature.

Handheld Dynamometers (HHDs)
HHDs provide a convenient alternative for measuring isometric strength in clinical and performance  
settings. While they are effective for assessing hip flexor, abductor and adductor strength, their accuracy 
and reliability largely depend on the standardization of the testing procedure.  
Some HHDs, such as the DynaMo range, also have in-built inclinometers, allowing practitioners to assess 
strength and range of motion (ROM) with a single device.
Fixed-Frame Dynamometers (FFDs)
In sports and rehabilitation settings, the use of FFDs has increased due to their portability and usability. 
They enable standardized isometric strength testing in multiple hip positions, making them valuable for 
assessing side-to-side differences in muscle strength and monitoring rehabilitation progress.

Force Plates
Force plates, such as ForceDecks, are commonly used to assess closed-environment biomechanics and 
jump performance during the rehabilitation and return-to-sport phases following hip and groin injury.  
By quantifying performance outcome metrics (e.g., jump height, peak force, rate of force development 
(RFD) and others) and strategy metrics (e.g., contraction time, countermovement depth and others), 
force plates can help identify performance impairments and side-to-side differences in lower limb 
strength and power, which can be used to inform training and rehabilitation practices.

The Hip and the Kinetic Chain
The hip serves as a central link in the kinetic chain, affecting pelvic stability, trunk control and lower-
limb mechanics. As previously discussed, functional limitations in mobility, strength and balance at 
the hip can lead to compensatory patterns, off-loading the hip and increasing the loads borne by the 
knees, ankles and lumbar spine. 
However, the opposite may also be true. Certain hip pathologies may come about not due to 
insufficient qualities within the hip and groin but rather due to a lack of capacity elsewhere in the 
kinetic chain. 
Therefore, leveraging holistic, technology-enabled assessments allows practitioners to uncover 
impairments that can be modified through the implementation of an individualized training or 
rehabilitation program.

ROM Testing
The assessment of hip ROM is another fundamental element of clinical assessment. Practitioners

Imaging
Imaging has become a central part of evaluating hip-related groin pain, especially with increased 
awareness of conditions like femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) and labral tears. However, 
overreliance on imaging risks mismanagement, unnecessary interventions and patient anxiety. 
While imaging advances have improved anatomical visualization, structural findings like cam 
morphology or labral tears are commonly seen in asymptomatic individuals. For example, over 65% 
of asymptomatic individuals may show labral tears, and more than half of athletes exhibit cam 
morphology without pain.
Therefore, imaging findings must be interpreted with caution and always in conjunction with clinical 
symptoms, history and physical examination.

Do Not Forget the Trunk and Upper Body 
Although the hip and groin are primary focus areas in assessment and training, it is essential to 
consider the body as a connected and integrated system. Strength and stability in the trunk and upper 
body are crucial for optimal hip and groin performance.  
In dynamic activities such as sprinting, cutting and kicking, the upper body and core contribute 
significantly to force production and movement control. Impaired proximal segment force production 
and movement control can result in increased load distribution across the hip and groin regions, 
thereby increasing their vulnerability to injury. 
Consequently, for athletes with hip and groin injuries, it is also important to assess the force 
production capacities of the trunk and upper body. Additionally, practitioners should also consider the 
contribution of the trunk to the performance of closed-environment tasks such as a single-leg squat.  
Focusing solely on building hip and groin strength while neglecting the trunk and upper body can lead 
to imbalances, ultimately impacting performance and elevating injury risk.

Flexion Adduction Internal Rotation (FADDIR) Test
With the patient supine, the examiner passively flexes the hip to 90° before moving it into end-range 
adduction and internal rotation. A positive test is indicated by pain or a clicking sensation, suggesting 
potential issues with the cartilage, labrum, capsule or signs of femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) 
and hip OA. 
Hip Quadrant Test
Also performed with the patient supine, the examiner flexes the hip to 90° while allowing comfortable 
knee flexion. By interlocking the fingers and cupping the patient’s knee, an adduction force is applied 
and the hip is then moved from 90° into full flexion – with possible added compression. Pain or clicking 
during this maneuver is considered a positive sign of intra-articular hip pathology. 
Flexion, Abduction and External Rotation (FABER) Test (Sacroiliac 
Joint Stress Test)
In this test, the patient lies supine with the foot of the test leg placed on the opposite knee. The 
examiner stabilizes the pelvis by pressing on the opposite anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) and 
slowly lowers the test leg’s knee toward the table until it reaches the table or aligns parallel with the 
opposite leg. Pain in the hip or sacroiliac region indicates a positive test. 
Trendelenburg Test for Abductor Dysfunction
The patient is asked to stand on one leg. A positive Trendelenburg sign is noted if the pelvis on the 
contralateral side drops, often accompanied by ipsilateral hip pain, indicating weakness or dysfunction 
of the hip abductors.

by a positive FABER and Ober test. The cluster of these findings – local tenderness, pain with specific 
movements and resistance tests – helps confirm the diagnosis. 
FADIR-R
Performed with the patient supine and the hip flexed to 90°, this test involves resistively testing IR. The 
emergence of lateral hip pain during the resisted motion signals a positive finding, suggesting lateral 
hip pathology. 
ADD-R Test
In a side-lying position, the patient’s top leg is adducted over the edge of the plinth. Resistance is 
then applied as the patient attempts to abduct the leg. The development of lateral hip pain during this 
maneuver is considered a positive sign.

Modified Thomas Test
The patient perches at the end of a plinth, brings the non-test leg to the chest and lies back so the test 
leg hangs off the end. If the hanging leg remains in hip flexion, it indicates tightness of the iliopsoas; if 
it abducts, tightness of the ITB and tensor fasciae latae (TFL) may be present; and if the knee extends 
beyond 90°, the rectus femoris is likely tight. 
 
Other Assessments
Isometric Abdominal Assessments 
The patient lies supine with hips and knees extended. The patient performs a sit-up movement, 
lifting the head and scapulae off the examination table. The examiner applies resistance by placing 
one hand on the patient’s knees and the other on the chest to maintain an isometric contraction. This 
test is considered positive if the patient experiences recognizable pain in the inguinal region during 
the maneuver.
